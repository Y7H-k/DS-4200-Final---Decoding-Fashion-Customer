About the project

Introduction

For a fashion retailer, every transaction is a data point, and 3,900 of them tell a story worth listening to. This project analyzes the Customer Shopping Behavior Analysis dataset, drawn from an e-commerce fashion retailer operating across all 50 U.S. states, to uncover the spending patterns and customer behaviors that drive revenue. The goal is simple: to give retailers a clearer picture of where their money is coming from, when demand peaks, and where there is room to grow.

The dataset covers the full scope of a retail transaction, including customer demographics, product categories, purchase amounts, seasonality, payment behavior, and more. With that range of variables, we can move past broad revenue summaries and start asking more targeted questions: Which categories are actually carrying the business? Are there seasons where certain products underperform and could benefit from a well-timed promotion? Which states and regions represent untapped demand? Which customer segments are spending the most?

We structured our analysis around 5 visualizations, each targeting a different layer of the story. We start geographically, mapping where sales are strongest across the country, before zooming in to how often customers come back for specific items. From there, we examine purchase frequency by item to understand which products keep customers coming back. We then look at how purchase amounts shift by season and category, it is a critical input for planning promotional calendars. A category-level revenue comparison establishes where the bulk of spending is concentrated. Finally, a treemap breaks down spending by gender, category, and item, surfacing the demographic patterns that should shape inventory and marketing decisions.

Taken together, these charts are designed to help a retailer answer the questions that matter most: where to invest, when to act, and who to target.

About the Dataset

Introduction to Data

We are working with the Customer Shopping Behavior Analysis dataset from Kaggle, which is a collection of 3,900 transactions from an e-commerce fashion retailer that ships across all 50 U.S. states. The data is clean with no missing values.

What makes the dataset particularly useful is how much it captures about each transaction. Beyond just what was bought and how much was spent, it tells us who the customer is, including their age, gender, and location, as well as the details of the purchase itself, like the product category, size, color, and the season it was made in. It even includes behavioral patterns, whether the customer is a subscriber, how often they shop, how they paid, and what they thought of their purchase afterward.

All together, the dataset has 5 numerical features: Customer ID, Age, Purchase Amount (USD), Review Rating, and Previous Purchases; and 11 Categorical features: Gender, Item Purchased, Category, Location, Size, Color, Season, Subscription Status, Discount Applied, Payment Method, Frequency of Payments. That mix of demographic, transactional, and behavioral data is what allows us to look at shopping patterns from multiple angles rather than just one.

Visualization 1 · Altair Map

Most Popular Purchase in U.S.

How to interact: Hover over any state to see the state name, total sale amount, most sold item, and its quantity. Towards the top right is a legend associated with the purchase amount in dollars, with a color gradient ranging from purple to yellow.

What this shows: This interactive map of the United States displays the total sales collected at the end of the year, aggregated across all transactions in the dataset. States are shaded along a color gradient to make it easy to identify high- and low-performing regions at a glance.

Key takeaways: Regional patterns in both sales volume and product preferences reveal clear geographic trends that can inform inventory and marketing decisions. Western states lead in revenue, while northeastern states share a preference for cold-weather clothing.

  • The company's best-selling states are concentrated in the west, including Montana, Idaho, Nevada, and California.
  • Adjacent states show a stark contrast in sales, with the biggest difference reaching up to $1,500 in purchase amount.
  • On the eastern side, sales are above the median on average, posing no immediate concern.
  • North Dakota, Montana, and Idaho's most sold items are warm clothing such as jackets and coats, likely driven by colder climates.
  • California and Nevada's top items are dresses and backpacks, reflecting a warmer climate and different lifestyle needs.
  • We should continue investing resources in western states while investigating the cause of the stark contrast between top-selling states and their neighbors.

Visualization 2 · D3.js Bar Chart

Total Revenue by Product Category

How to interact: Hover over any bar to see the exact revenue total for that category. Click any bar to drill down into an item-level ranking showing how revenue is distributed across individual products within that category. Click ← Back to Categories to return to the main view.

What this shows: This bar chart shows the total purchase amount in USD for each of the four product categories, including Clothing, Accessories, Footwear, and Outwear, aggregated across all 3,900 transactions in the dataset. The bars are sorted from highest to lowest revenue, making it easy to see where customer spending is concentrated at a glance. When we hover over any bar, we will see the exact total for that category.

  • Clothing is the dominant category at $104,264, nearly 45% of total revenue, making it the clear revenue engine of the business.
  • Accessories follow at $74,200 (~32%), meaning Clothing and Accessories together account for over 76% of all spending.
  • Footwear contributes $36,093 (15.5%), while Outerwear trails at just $18,524 (under 8%); this shows that customers spend more than 5× as much on Clothing as on Outerwear.
  • Within Clothing, Blouse, Shirt, and Dress lead item revenue (each above $10,300), while Jeans underperform at $7,548, which is nearly 30% less than the top Clothing items.
  • Within Accessories, revenue is more evenly spread across items ($8,477–$10,010), suggesting no single accessory dominates demand.
  • Outerwear's low share likely reflects its seasonal and infrequent purchase nature, but also raises the question of whether it is being marketed effectively or priced appropriately.

Key takeaways: For a fashion retailer, this signals a clear priority: Clothing and Accessories are the revenue engines and deserve the most inventory investment and promotional attention. Outwear’s low share likely reflects its seasonal and infrequent purchase nature, but also raises the question of whether it is being marketed effectively or priced appropriately to drive more volume.

Visualization 3 · Ploty express Treemap

Revenue Breakdown by Gender, Category and Item

How to interact: Hover over any rectangle to view the item name, its position in the hierarchy (gender → category → item), and the average purchase amount.

What this shows: This treemap shows total transactions broken down hierarchically by gender, product category, and individual item. Rectangle size represents the number of transactions, while color intensity represents the average purchase amount per transaction — darker shades indicate lower spending and brighter shades indicate higher spending. This allows the retailer to compare both purchase volume and spending behavior across items and genders.

Key takeaways:

  • Male customers account for significantly higher transaction volume than females (2,652 vs. 1,248), and this pattern holds across all categories.
  • Higher volume does not always mean higher spending. For males, T-shirts have the highest average purchase amount (~$64) with 101 transactions, while Jewelry has a lower average (~$55) despite a higher count (119).
  • For females, Dresses have the highest average purchase amount (~$67) with 52 transactions, while Sandals have the lowest (~$53) with 59 transactions, showing that higher-priced items can still have strong demand.
  • The same item can vary in price across genders. Jewelry averages ~$64 for females compared to ~$55 for males, even though more males purchase it.
  • Large differences in purchase volume appear across items, such as Sneakers (103 males vs. 42 females) and Backpacks (106 males vs. 37 females), indicating distinct gender preferences.

Visualization 4 · Altair Boxplot

Purchase Amount Distribution by Season & Category

How to interact: Use the Gender dropdown to switch between All, Male, and Female customers. Use the Category dropdown to isolate a single product type for a cleaner seasonal comparison. Hover over any box to see the exact median, Q1, Q3, and full range for that group.

What this shows: This boxplot shows how customer purchase amounts are distributed across the four seasons, broken down by product category. Each box covers the middle 50% of spending (Q1 to Q3), with the line marking the median. The whiskers extend to the full observed range. A wide IQR means customers in that group are making purchases across a broad price range, which is just as useful to a retailer as knowing the typical spend, because it signals inconsistent demand that targeted promotions can address.

  • Overall, median purchase amounts are remarkably stable across all seasons, ranging from $51 to $65, no single season is dramatically cheaper or more expensive for shoppers overall.
  • Clothing peaks in Winter (median $63) and dips in Summer ($55), consistent with customers investing in heavier, pricier pieces as temperatures drop, signaling Summer as the right time for Clothing promotions.
  • Accessories have their highest medians in Summer ($63) and Fall ($62), likely driven by seasonal items like sunglasses and scarves, making those the strongest seasons for Accessories marketing.
  • Footwear spending is highest in Fall ($64) and Winter ($63), dropping to $58–$59 in Spring and Summer, reflecting boots and heavier shoe purchases as temperatures fall.
  • Outerwear consistently has the lowest medians across all seasons ($51–$58.50) and the most compressed IQRs, confirming it is bought infrequently and at lower price points on average, making it the category most in need of targeted promotional activity.
  • Female Outerwear in Spring and Winter shows a noticeably wider IQR than Male, suggesting more varied spending behavior in that segment worth investigating.

Key takeaways: For a retailer, the key insight is that seasonal promotions should be category-specific: a targeted discount on Clothing in Summer or Outerwear in Spring, when spending dips or variance is high, is likely more effective than a blanket seasonal sale.

Visualization 5 · Altair Heatmap

Frequency of purchases for each item purchased

How to interact: Use the Category dropdown to filter by Clothing, Accessories, Footwear, or Outerwear. Hover over any cell to view the item name, purchase frequency, and exact customer count.

What this shows: This heatmap shows how often customers purchase each of the 25 items in the dataset, from weekly to annually. Color intensity represents the number of customers in each item–frequency combination, with darker shades indicating lower counts and brighter shades indicating higher counts. This allows the retailer to quickly identify which items are purchased frequently and which are more seasonal.

Key takeaways:

  • Sunglasses have the highest demand on the chart (36 customers quarterly), making them the strongest seasonal item and a clear opportunity for targeted promotions.
  • Pants and Blouses both have 32 customers, but Pants are purchased monthly while Blouses are annual, indicating steady demand versus seasonal demand and different restocking needs.
  • Boots show the largest frequency gap (31 quarterly vs. 8 monthly), suggesting strongly seasonal demand rather than consistent purchases.
  • Jackets have more evenly distributed demand (29 every 3 months vs. 19 weekly), but still show a seasonal pattern that benefits from planned restocking.
  • Purchase behavior varies significantly by item, meaning demand is not uniform across products.

Conclusion

Through our analysis, it’d be best to introduce a loyalty program for our most frequent customers to further incentivize repeat purchases. We’ve also found that we have twice as many male customers than female, yet both contribute about the same amount of revenue as each other. To further expand revenue across all categories, we recommend partnering with influencers or launching campaigns that showcase complete wardrobe looks that combine multiple categories together to drive purchase beyond what customers would buy on their own.

Geographically, western states are leading in sales, so continuing to invest in successful strategies should be a priority. However, resources should also be directed toward investigating why neighboring states are underperforming despite their proximity.

With products, clothing and accessories have the highest sales during the Fall and Winter, making seasonal campaigns an opportunity worth pursuing. Outerwear, which only totals about 8% of sales, should be experimented with to stimulate demand. Finally, since spending habits and purchasing rhythms vary too widely, item-specific demographic-aware planning should be adopted to better capture customer behavior for future strategy.
