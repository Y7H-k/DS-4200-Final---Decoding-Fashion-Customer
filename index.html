About the project

Introduction

For a fashion retailer, every transaction is a data point, and 3,900 of them tell a story worth listening to. This project analyzes the Customer Shopping Behavior Analysis dataset, drawn from an e-commerce fashion retailer operating across all 50 U.S. states, to uncover the spending patterns and customer behaviors that drive revenue. The goal is straightforward: give retailers a clearer picture of where their money is coming from, when demand peaks, and where there's room to grow.

The dataset covers the full scope of a retail transaction, including customer demographics, product categories, purchase amounts, seasonality, payment behavior, and more. With that range of variables, we can move past broad revenue summaries and start asking more targeted questions: Which categories are actually carrying the business? Are there seasons where certain products underperform and could benefit from a well-timed promotion? Which states and regions represent untapped demand? Which customer segments are spending the most?

We structured our analysis around 5 visualizations, each targeting a different layer of the story. We start geographically, mapping where sales are strongest across the country, before zooming in to how often customers come back for specific items. From there, we examine purchase frequency by item to understand which products keep customers coming back. We then look at how purchase amounts shift by season and category, it is a critical input for planning promotional calendars. A category-level revenue comparison establishes where the bulk of spending is concentrated. Finally, a treemap breaks down spending by gender, category, and item, surfacing the demographic patterns that should shape inventory and marketing decisions.

Taken together, these charts are designed to help a retailer answer the questions that matter most: where to invest, when to act, and who to target.

About the Dataset

Introduction to Data

We are working with the Customer Shopping Behavior Analysis dataset from Kaggle, which is a collection of 3,900 transactions from an e-commerce fashion retailer that ships across all 50 U.S. states. The data is clean with no missing values.

What makes the dataset particularly useful is how much it captures about each transaction. Beyond just what was bought and how much was spent, it tells us who the customer is, including their age, gender, and location, as well as the details of the purchase itself, like the product category, size, color, and the season it was made in. It even includes behavioral patterns, whether the customer is a subscriber, how often they shop, how they paid, and what they thought of their purchase afterward.

All together, the dataset has 5 numerical features: Customer ID, Age, Purchase Amount (USD), Review Rating, and Previous Purchases; and 11 Categorical features: Gender, Item Purchased, Category, Location, Size, Color, Season, Subscription Status, Discount Applied, Payment Method, Frequency of Payments. That mix of demographic, transactional, and behavioral data is what allows us to look at shopping patterns from multiple angles rather than just one.

Visualization 1 · Altair Map

Most Popular Purchase in U.S.

The following is an interactive map of the United States, showing the total sales collected at the end of the year. As a mouse hovers over a state, the state name, sale amount, most sold item, and its quantity are revealed. Towards the top right is a legend associated with the purchase amount in dollars, with a color gradient starting from purple to yellow.

Displaying purchase amounts throughout each state could reveal a pattern among regions or adjacent states, and the most popular item could display patterns that we want to better prepare for in the future to keep such items in stock. It also gives a good indication of which states we’re excelling at and which states we may need to reconsider our strategy for achieving higher sales.

From the map, we can see the company's best-selling states are in the west, consisting of Montana, Idaho, Nevada, and California. Adjacent to these states is a stark contrast in how much is being sold, with the biggest difference being a $1,500 difference. On the eastern side, we see that the sales are above the median on average. North Dakota, Montana, and Idaho have the most sold clothing that are meant to keep warm, as seen with jackets and coats, whereas California and Nevada have the most sold dresses and backpacks. The adjacent states follow no pattern except being a stark contrast from our highest-selling states. Northeastern states have a common interest in clothing to keep warm, likely due to the cold temperatures throughout the year.

The state overview of sales of its top items tells us we’re selling the most in the western states and have a sharp decline in purchase amount in its adjacent states; the states elsewhere are averaging around the median of purchase amount, which doesn’t pose a concern. We should continue to spend additional resources on the western states while investigating the cause of the stark contrast in purchase amount between the top-selling western states and their adjacent states.

Visualization 2 · D3.js Bar Chart

Total Revenue by Product Category

Before diving into more detailed breakdowns by season, gender, or item, it is important to establish a baseline: which categories actually drive revenue? This chart answers that directly. Understanding the revenue split across categories tells a business where its core demand is coming from, and which categories may be underperforming relative to their potential. It also sets the context for every other visualization on the page. When we later examine seasonal patterns or spending distributions, knowing that Clothing accounts for nearly half of revenue makes our findings more meaningful.

This bar chart shows the total purchase amount in USD for each of the four product categories, including Clothing, Accessories, Footwear, and Outerwear, aggregated across all 3,900 transactions in the dataset. The bars are sorted from highest to lowest revenue, making it easy to see where customer spending is concentrated at a glance. When we hover over any bar, we will see the exact total for that category.

Clothing is the dominant revenue category by a significant margin, generating $104,264 in total purchases, it is nearly 45% of all revenue across the dataset. Accessories follow at $74,220, about 32%, making these two categories together responsible for over three-quarters of total spending. Footwear contributes $36,093 (15.5%), while Outerwear trails considerably at just $18,524 (less than 8%) of total revenue. The gap between Clothing and Outerwear is striking: customers spend more than 5 times as much on Clothing as they do on Outerwear. For a fashion retailer, this signals a clear priority: Clothing and Accessories are the revenue engines and deserve the most inventory investment and promotional attention. Outerwear's low share likely reflects its seasonal and infrequent purchase nature, but also raises the question of whether it is being marketed effectively or priced appropriately to drive more volume.

Revenue is heavily concentrated in Clothing and Accessories, which together account for over 76% of all customer spending. Footwear holds a moderate share, while Outerwear is a distant last. A retailer looking to grow overall revenue should focus on deepening its Clothing and Accessories offerings, while evaluating whether Outerwear's low contribution reflects a structural limitation or a missed opportunity worth addressing through targeted promotions or expanded selection.

Visualization 3 · Altair Boxplot

Purchase Amount Distribution by Season & Category

This boxplot shows how customer purchase amounts are distributed across the four seasons, broken down by product category. Each box covers the middle 50% of spending (Q1 to Q3), with the white line marking the median. The whiskers extend to the full observed range. Use the Gender dropdown to switch between male, female, or all customers, and the Category dropdown to isolate a single product type for a cleaner seasonal view. Hovering over any box reveals the exact median, quartiles, and range for that group.

Purchase amount is the most direct signal of how much customers are actually spending, and we wanted to understand whether that changes depending on the season or what they're buying. This boxplot lets us see both the typical spend and how spread out it is. A wide IQR means customers in that group are making purchases across a broad price range, which is just as useful to a retailer as knowing the median.

Across all categories and genders, median purchase amounts are notably stable throughout the year, ranging from $51 to $65 across all season-category combinations. No single season is dramatically cheaper or more expensive for shoppers overall. That said, category-level patterns are worth paying attention to. Clothing spending peaks in Winter (median $63) and dips in Summer ($55), consistent with customers investing in heavier, pricier pieces as temperatures drop. Accessories follow a similar arc, with the highest medians in Summer ($63) and Fall ($62), likely driven by seasonal items like sunglasses and scarves. Footwear follows a seasonal pattern consistent with weather, median spending is highest in Fall ($64) and Winter ($63), dropping to $58–$59 in Spring and Summer. Customers tend to spend more on footwear as temperatures drop, likely reflecting purchases of boots and heavier shoes. Outerwear has the lowest medians across all seasons (ranging from $51 in Summer to $58.5 in Fall), and its spending is most inconsistent in Spring and Summer when it is least expected. For a retailer, the key insight is that seasonal promotions should be category-specific: a targeted discount on Clothing in Summer or Outerwear in Spring, when spending dips or variance is high, is likely more effective than a blanket seasonal sale.

Purchase amounts remain broadly stable across seasons, but the category-level view reveals where the real opportunities lie. Clothing and Accessories both peak in the second half of the year, making Fall and Winter the most valuable seasons for those categories. Footwear spending becomes most unpredictable in Winter, suggesting an opportunity for clearer price-point messaging. Outerwear consistently underperforms relative to other categories and would benefit from targeted promotions, particularly in seasons where customers are least likely to shop for it unprompted.

Visualization 4 · Altair Heatmap

Frequency of purchases for each item purchased

This heatmap shows how often the 25 items in the dataset are purchased by customers. The items are broken down by their reported shopping frequency, from weekly to annually. The number of customers is represented by each cell’s color intensity in that frequency combination. Hence, brighter yellow means a higher number of customers, and darker teal means fewer customers. The default view of the map shows all 25 items. However, there is a Category dropdown filter that allows users to choose a single product category, including clothing, accessories, footwear, or outerwear, for simpler comprehension. The tooltip in this interactive map allows the user to hover over any cell, which displays the item name, frequency band, and the exact number of customers. This provides the user with precise information along with the color gradient visuals.

When creating an inventory plan and marketing strategy, understanding what and how much of an item a customer buys frequently is helpful. The retailer is able to know not just what items sell, but also how frequently they sell. An item that shows high purchase frequency weekly or biweekly tells the retailer that the item needs reliable restocking, and an item that falls under the annual or quarterly basis indicates it is not demanded as much. Thus, this heatmap allows the retailer and user to learn the patterns in customer demand with the filter that helps in filtering data to tell a more specific story.

Filtering by category reveals that different items follow different purchasing rhythms. Under Clothing, Blouses, and Pants are both purchased by 32 customers, but at different frequencies (annually and monthly). This means that the retailer must restock pants since they are bought consistently every month, while blouses might need a bigger restock, but only during a certain time of the year. Jeans are bought by only 9 customers biweekly, which means almost nobody is buying jeans every two weeks. Filtering for Footwear shows that boots are bought quarterly by 31 customers, while only 8 customers buy boots monthly. This is the largest gap of any item on the chart, so it indicates that boots are popular in demand but only every quarter, and purchases may be season-driven. The category for Outerwear shows that jackets are purchased by 29 customers every 3 months, but also by 19 customers weekly. Here, there is a narrower gap, which means Outerwear demand is more evenly spread, but the retailer would still benefit from heavier restocking seasonally. Under Accessories, we can see that 36 customers buy Sunglasses every quarter, which is the single brightest cell on the entire heatmap. While only 11 people buy backpacks every month. This means Sunglasses have high seasonal demand worth building promotions around, while backpacks are not as necessary to customers. Overall, each item has its own purchasing rhythm, so the retailer should tailor restocking and promotions individually rather than applying one blanket strategy.

The heatmap shows that each item has its own purchasing rhythm — Pants need monthly restocking, Blouses need seasonal pushes, Boots are quarter-driven, and Sunglasses have the highest seasonal demand on the chart. The category filter and tooltip help the retailer see and confirm these patterns, which means restocking and promotions should be tailored at the item level rather than applied as one blanket strategy.

Visualization 5 · Altair Treemap

Revenue Breakdown by Gender, Category and Item

This plot is a treemap that provides a hierarchical view of total transactions, first broken down by gender (male and female), then by product category (Clothing, Accessories, Footwear, and Outerwear), and further by individual item. The number of transactions is represented by the size of the rectangles for that item within its gender and product category. The average purchase amount per transaction is encoded by each rectangle's color intensity. Hence, brighter yellow means a higher average spending, and darker teal means lower average spending. By hovering over the map, the tooltip allows the user to get further and clearer information about the item name, its position in the hierarchy, and the purchase amount

To further dive into revenue totals and different categories, the treemap helps the retailer understand spending behavior and purchasing patterns in females and males, especially for individual items. This allows the retailer to make evidence-based decisions when doing targeted marketing and product development. The single view of the hierarchy of gender, category, and items makes it easy to understand the overall item preferences in both genders and to see where there may be gaps to fill.

The treemap shows that male and female customers have very different transaction volumes, but higher volume doesn't always mean higher spending per purchase. Males are higher by almost 1,000 people versus females (2,652 vs 1,248), and the same is true across all item categories. The color gradient shows clearly that in males, T-shirts are the most expensive item (about $64) with a count of 101, and Jewelry is the least expensive (about $55) with a count of 119. This means both items are high in demand, and jewelry is a cheaper purchase compared to T-shirts. On the female side, Dresses are the most expensive (about $67) with a count of 52, while Sandals are the least expensive(about $53) with a count of 59. it means females still prefer to buy dresses even though they might be more expensive. It is also interesting that the same item can have different prices across genders: Jewelry for women is about $64 versus $55 for males, even though 119 males buy it compared to only 52 females. Items like Sneakers (42 women vs 103 men) and Backpacks (37 women vs 106 men) also show large gender gaps in volume. Overall, this means the retailer should not just look at which items sell the most but also consider who is buying them and how much they are spending per transaction.

The treemap shows that males dominate in transaction volume across all categories, but the color gradient reveals that volume and spending per purchase do not always go hand in hand. The retailer should consider not just which items sell the most, but also who is buying them and at what price point, to make better decisions about stocking and marketing for each gender.

Conclusions

Conclusion

Through our analysis, it’d be best to introduce a loyalty program for our most frequent customers to further incentivize repeat purchases. We’ve also found that we have twice as many male customers than female, yet both contribute about the same amount of revenue as each other. To further expand revenue across all categories, we recommend partnering with influencers or launching campaigns that showcase complete wardrobe looks that combine multiple categories together to drive purchase beyond what customers would buy on their own.

Geographically, western states are leading in sales, so continuing to invest in successful strategies should be a priority. However, resources should also be directed toward investigating why neighboring states are underperforming despite their proximity.

With products, clothing and accessories have the highest sales during the Fall and Winter, making seasonal campaigns an opportunity worth pursuing. Outerwear, which only totals about 8% of sales, should be experimented with to stimulate demand. Finally, since spending habits and purchasing rhythms vary too widely, item-specific demographic-aware planning should be adopted to better capture customer behavior for future strategy.
